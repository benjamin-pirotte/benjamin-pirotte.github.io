## Why I made the switch

I spent six years as a front-end developer before transitioning into product management at Collibra. I loved writing code, but I kept finding myself more interested in the *why* than the *how* — why are we building this feature? Who actually needs it? What would make it truly useful?

When a PM role opened up internally, I jumped at it. What followed was one of the steepest learning curves of my career.

## What surprised me

### Influence without authority is hard

As an engineer, you have clear deliverables. As a PM, you're responsible for outcomes you can't directly control. You don't manage the engineers, designers, or data scientists — but you're accountable for what the team ships.

Learning to lead through trust, context, and persuasion rather than instructions was a fundamental shift. It requires a different kind of patience.

### The hardest skill is saying no

The backlog is always infinite. Stakeholders always have urgent requests. The temptation is to say yes to everything and let the team figure it out.

This is a trap. The most valuable thing a PM does is create clarity — and that means ruthlessly protecting the team's focus. Every yes is a no to something else. Getting comfortable with that took me a long time.

### Your technical background is an asset — use it carefully

Coming from engineering, I had credibility with developers and could speak their language. That was genuinely useful. But I also had to resist the urge to dive into implementation details. My job was to define the *what* and the *why*, not the *how*.

The shift is from "I could build this a better way" to "what's the outcome we're trying to achieve?"

## What actually helped

- **Talking to users obsessively.** Not surveys. Actual conversations. The feedback that shapes a product is almost never in a spreadsheet.
- **Writing things down.** Good PMs create clarity through writing — PRDs, decision docs, one-pagers. Writing forces precision.
- **Finding a mentor inside product.** Technical chops can only take you so far. A good PM mentor accelerates the rest.

## The honest truth

Five years in, I still have weeks where I wonder if I made the right call. That uncertainty never fully goes away — and I've come to think that's actually healthy. It keeps you curious, close to users, and honest about what you don't know.
